My short reflection after my first internship.

It started in November 2025, right after the end of Semester 2 and my exams. I took a quick trip to Korea for about two weeks, then came back for a summer internship.

Through CSIRO, I had the chance to work as a research intern at Pawsey Supercomputing Centre.

Throughout 2025 I tried my best with job applications, I really wanted to gain experience related to what I had learned so far. It's rewarding, and the thing I realised is: if I can help someone with my knowledge or skills, like when I was doing my capstone and helping out FOWI (I actually need to write another diary about my capstone, what went well, what went wrong, etc.), it's just so fulfilling. I could genuinely feel happy in those moments.

Anyway, I actually counted.

I sent about ~50 different applications. I got lots of “no, sorry” emails, and I just kept going, working, studying, attending events and webinars, updating my resume, getting an AWS certificate, etc.

That little bit of effort really did work, and I got the call from Pawsey.

Anyway, looking back now, I feel like I'm really lucky to have gotten this opportunity.

It was a really well-organised system: one week of intern training, getting paid while learning, and learning so many new areas too, HPC, computational chemistry, agentic AI, and more. It's such a valuable experience.

They also ran workshops for interns, including sessions on how to communicate scientific research effectively. I'm really grateful for opportunities like this where they invest in students, it makes me feel motivated to work even harder.

I met a lot of people too, and it's a memory I won't forget.

And with the CSIRO benefits, I took courses on O'Reilly like agentic AI and software architecture, and they helped me a lot.

I am from a computer science background, not software engineering, so I lack a bit of software testing, architecture, and that kind of stuff, I could use this chance to catch up on the theory.

Now Claude Opus 4.6 is out, and you can even control apps on your computer with OpenClaw, but my conclusion is: it's still not enough to do 100% vibe coding yet.

I don't think software engineering is dead, it's just that far fewer people are needed now to "write code" because of the rapid advancements in AI,  
but core areas like software architecture and system design are still very valuable.

The singularity, where AGI or whatever comes out and exceeds human intelligence (this is something I want to write a whole post about too) will be the point where no human software engineers are needed.

Will this time ever come?
It feels like something on the level of using a quantum computer without errors.
